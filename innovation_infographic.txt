                </div>
                <span class="author-pill">Proto-National Innovation System</span>
              </div>
              <p class="author-summary">
                Innovation as a national project: building productive forces, not only
                accumulating capital.
              </p>
              <div class="author-detail">
                <ul>
                  <li>Stressed the role of <em>productive forces</em>: education, transport,
                      industry and institutions.</li>
                  <li>Argued that the State must nurture strategic sectors to enable
                      long-term learning and upgrading.</li>
                  <li>Shifted the focus from individual inventors to
                      <strong>national capabilities</strong> and policy.</li>

            <div class="author-card" data-author="babbage">

                <div class="author-main">
                  <span class="author-name">Charles Babbage</span>
                  <span class="author-tag">Organisation of production</span>
                </div>
                <span class="author-pill">Learning inside the factory</span>
              </div>
              <p class="author-summary">
                Showed how division of labour and factory routines generate
                cumulative technical knowledge.
              </p>
              <div class="author-detail">
                <ul>
                  <li>Linked <strong>organisation of work</strong> with the emergence of new
                      skills and process innovations.</li>
                  <li>Highlighted feedback loops between machinery, workers’ know-how
                      and management practices.</li>
                  <li>Anticipated later ideas about firms as <em>repositories of knowledge</em>.</li>
                </ul>

            <div class="author-card" data-author="thunen">

                  <span class="author-tag">Spatial context &amp; incentives</span>
                </div>
                <span class="author-pill">Environment &amp; diffusion</span>
              </div>
              <p class="author-summary">
                Connected innovation with location, transport costs and incentives
                along the territory.
              </p>
              <div class="author-detail">
                <ul>
                  <li>Showed how <strong>space and distance</strong> shape which techniques are
                      viable and profitable.</li>
                  <li>Suggested that new methods diffuse unevenly across regions,
                      depending on infrastructure and markets.</li>
                  <li>Helps us read innovation as territorially embedded, not neutral.</li>
                </ul>
              </div>

        <!-- RIGHT: INSIGHTS -->
        <article class="insights-card">
          <h2 class="section-title">Key insights for innovation policy</h2>
          <p class="section-body">
            Hover each block to reveal how these nineteenth-century ideas connect
            to current debates on <strong>innovation systems</strong> and <strong>public policy</strong>.
          </p>

          <div class="insight-list">
            <div class="insight-item">
              <strong>1. Innovation is systemic, not purely individualistic</strong>
              List, Babbage and von Thünen already treated innovation as the outcome
              of interactions among firms, workers, infrastructure and the State.
            </div>

            <div class="insight-item">
              <strong>2. Capabilities are built, not given</strong>
              They anticipated the modern focus on capability building:
              education, technical skills, transport networks and industrial
              ecosystems as pre-conditions for innovation.
            </div>

            <div class="insight-item">
              <strong>3. The State as enabler of collective learning</strong>
              Rather than “picking winners” in a narrow sense, these authors point
              to the State as architect of the institutional and physical
              environment where learning and experimentation become viable.
            </div>

            <div class="insight-item">
              <strong>4. Innovation is cumulative and path-dependent</strong>
              Innovation emerges through gradual, path-dependent processes in which
              past investments, routines and spatial patterns shape future options.
            </div>
          </div>
